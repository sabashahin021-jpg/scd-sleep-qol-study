Exploring subjective cognitive decline, sleep patterns, and lifestyle determinants in urban North Indian population

A survey-based cross-sectional study

Saba Shahin, Dr Hammad Ahmad Siddiqui (PT), Prof. (Dr) Sohrab Ahmad Khan
Department of Physiotherapy
School of Allied Health Sciences and Research (SAHSR)
Jamia Hamdard (Deemed to be University), New Delhi
About this study
Problem statement

Cognitive decline in mid-life is a growing public health concern, with over 55 million people living with dementia globally and India carrying one of the largest burdens. Subjective cognitive decline, the self-perceived worsening of memory and thinking before any measurable deficit appears on tests, offers a window for early identification. However, North Indian urban populations have not been studied for SCD prevalence or its relationship with sleep quality and quality of life. Middle-aged adults between 40 and 60 years are frequently exposed to poor sleep, physical inactivity, and metabolic risk, yet routine cognitive screening in Indian primary care is not standard practice. No survey-based study has examined how sleep quality and lifestyle factors together relate to SCD in this specific population, leaving a gap in locally relevant, actionable data.

Aim

To produce locally relevant, evidence-based data on cognitive self-perception, sleep quality, and quality of life among urban North Indian adults aged 40 to 60 years, and to inform how subjective cognitive decline might be identified and addressed in primary care settings.

Objectives
1To assess the distribution and burden of subjective cognitive decline (SCD), measured using SCD-Q21, among middle-aged adults aged 40 to 60 years in an urban North Indian population.
2To assess the association between sleep quality, measured using the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI), and SCD in this group.
3To evaluate the relationship between SCD and health-related quality of life across physical, psychological, social, and environmental domains using the WHOQOL-BREF.
4To assess cognitive status using the Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA) and to examine whether self-reported cognitive complaints correspond with objective cognitive performance.
Study design

Cross-sectional, survey-based, community-level study conducted in urban North India (New Delhi).

Study period

2024-2025. Data collected through interviewer-assisted structured questionnaires from 150 participants (125 retained after exclusion).

Instruments used
SCD-Q21

Subjective Cognitive Decline Questionnaire. 21 items, score 0-21. Higher scores = more cognitive complaints. Covers memory, attention, language, executive function.

MoCA

Montreal Cognitive Assessment. Objective screening, score 0-30. Higher = better cognition. Score below 26 = screening cutoff for mild cognitive impairment.

PSQI

Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index. Global score 0-21. Higher = worse sleep. Score above 5 = poor sleep quality (Buysse et al., 1989).

WHOQOL-BREF

WHO Quality of Life. 4 domains (Physical, Psychological, Social, Environment) scaled 0-100. Two standalone items (Overall QOL, General Health) rated 1-5.

Key findings
The mean SCD-Q21 score was 8.22 out of 21, indicating mild to moderate subjective cognitive complaints. About a third of participants scored 12 or above.
The mean MoCA score (22.02) fell below the screening cutoff of 26, suggesting possible cognitive impairment across the sample.
70.4% of participants (88 of 125) were classified as poor sleepers based on the PSQI cutoff of 5.
Higher SCD-Q21 scores correlated significantly with lower MoCA (r = -0.333), worse physical health (r = -0.298), lower psychological wellbeing (r = -0.318), poorer general health (r = -0.244), and weaker social relationships (r = -0.176).
Higher PSQI scores correlated significantly with worse physical health (r = -0.428), lower psychological wellbeing (r = -0.311), poorer environment (r = -0.228), lower overall QOL (r = -0.182), lower MoCA (r = -0.172), and poorer general health (r = -0.173).
SCD-Q21 and PSQI were not significantly correlated (r = +0.089, p = 0.277), suggesting subjective cognitive decline and sleep quality are independent constructs in this age group.
All WHOQOL-BREF domains were positively intercorrelated, with the strongest link between psychological and environment (r = 0.497, p < 0.001).
